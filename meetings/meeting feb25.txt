Goal for MVP Development: The primary objective of the discussion was to scope a pathway for developing a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) of a mystery shopping agent, leveraging existing work, particularly around digital personas. The project aims to utilize a digital persona as the first real use case for this technology, requiring collaboration to integrate it with ongoing work led by Kate and CLA (00:00:00).
Problem Statement: Price Competitiveness: The business currently tracks price competitiveness using aggregated price indices, which are crucial for setting pricing. Recent brand perception surveys have indicated a deterioration in price perception among shoppers relative to competitors, such as Kohl's (00:01:25).
Limitations of Aggregate Pricing Metrics: The current aggregated approach to measuring price competitiveness may obscure variance experienced by individual customers, as demonstrated by a pre-Christmas example where a specific recipe shop cost 50% more at Woollies than at a competitor. This discrepancy occurred because the company did not carry certain on-brand SKUs, leading to a challenge for the customer team to create a comparative pricing view purely from a customer's perspective (00:02:30).
Manual Shopping Basket Experiment: An initial manual effort involved creating eight shopping baskets, linking them to specific customer profiles, and having people shop them in Woolly's, Kohl's, and Aldi to understand the customer experience. This single-sample test indicated that the company was more expensive in all but two baskets, but the process required three people and an hour and a half each to complete only two baskets, proving it was not a scalable solution (00:03:53).
Proposal for Digital Agent Solution: The solution proposed is to use an agent, leveraging digital personas, to virtually shop these baskets at scale (00:03:53). The agent would be primed with a customer profile and the generic item needed for a shopping mission, then required to select the specific SKU based on the profile's values (e.g., quality, price), simulating customer decision-making (00:05:16).
Challenges in Basket Construction and Data Granularity: The conversation highlighted challenges in constructing representative baskets, noting that internal data exists on current customer shopping habits but not on baskets lost to competitors (00:08:55). The discussion also addressed the need for scalability, moving beyond the initial eight baskets and two segments to achieve a more robust data set for drawing actionable conclusions (00:07:48).
Addressing Price Difference Attribution: It was acknowledged that observed price differences could be attributed to various factors beyond simple price, such as substitution, availability, promotions, packaging, or collectibles (00:10:25). To gain real insight, the focus must be on the underlying factors driving the price delta, such as pack sizes, rather than just the difference itself (00:12:52).
Strategic Approaches for Basket Construction: Two main strategies for basket construction were discussed: a personalized approach using individual customer mission data to build a digital twin basket for comparison across retailers, or a segment-based approach using representative "lookalike" baskets mapped to groups (e.g., a "school mom") (00:16:12). It was suggested that the agent should make SKU decisions based on the mission and list, similar to a human, to avoid internal arguments over exact basket compositions and better reflect customer choices (00:18:50).
Combining Internal and External Data: Although the company has rich internal data on its customers, the challenge remains in overlaying competitor data, which necessitates approximating the customer experience at other retailers. It was suggested that a combination of internal data and the agent's behavioral lens is necessary to address the competitive gaps (00:21:09).
Mission-Based vs. Data-Driven Basket Design: While a bottom-up, data-driven approach to constructing key baskets was initially attempted, the team shifted to a descriptive, mission-based approach (e.g., shopping for ingredients for spaghetti bolognese) because it was easier for executives to understand and the missions were interchangeable across retailers (00:22:31). This method allows the agent to make decisions about specific products based on the persona's priorities (brand, price, etc.) (00:24:47).
Need for an Iterative Development Path: It was agreed that the process must be iterative, starting with the manual in-store test to prove the concept, moving to building a more efficient digital tool, and then scaling it to different baskets and approximating inter-retailer dynamics (00:28:36). The tool's primary goal is to reflect how customers make decisions, moving beyond purely statistical analysis (00:27:05).
